PART 1: PER-LOBE E:I VARIATION
================================================================================

Each lobe shows different patterns of E:I heterogeneity across its subregions:

OCCIPITAL LOBE (rCMRGlc = 35.8):
  Regions: V1C, V2, A19 (n=3)
  Mean E:I: 3.28 ± 0.82
  Range: 2.50 - 4.42 (span: 1.92)
  Coefficient of Variation: 25.0% (HIGHEST - most heterogeneous)

  Interpretation: V1C (4.42) is a major outlier, much higher than V2 (2.94)
  and A19 (2.50). This suggests primary visual cortex has unique E:I balance
  despite sharing metabolic rate with other occipital regions.

FRONTAL LOBE (rCMRGlc = 35.3):
  Regions: M1C, A13, A25, A32, A44-45, A46 (n=6)
  Mean E:I: 2.37 ± 0.47
  Range: 1.48 - 3.05 (span: 1.56)
  Coefficient of Variation: 20.0% (second most heterogeneous)

  Interpretation: Wide variation from A32 (1.48) to M1C (3.05). Primary motor
  cortex (M1C) has high E:I similar to V1C, while dorsal ACC (A32) has very
  low E:I. Frontal lobe is functionally diverse, reflected in E:I variation.

TEMPORAL LOBE (rCMRGlc = 30.5):
  Regions: MTG, STG, ITG, A38, A1C (n=5)
  Mean E:I: 2.41 ± 0.39
  Range: 1.73 - 2.87 (span: 1.15)
  Coefficient of Variation: 16.2%

  Interpretation: Moderate heterogeneity. MTG (2.87) highest, ITG (1.73) lowest.

INSULAR LOBE (rCMRGlc = 30.3):
  Regions: FI, Ig (n=2)
  Mean E:I: 2.76 ± 0.37
  Range: 2.39 - 3.13 (span: 0.74)
  Coefficient of Variation: 13.5%

  Interpretation: Moderate variation with only 2 regions. Ig (3.13) notably
  higher than FI (2.39).

PARIETAL LOBE (rCMRGlc = 35.8):
  Regions: S1C, A5-A7, A40, A43 (n=4)
  Mean E:I: 2.36 ± 0.19
  Range: 2.04 - 2.49 (span: 0.45)
  Coefficient of Variation: 7.9% (LOWEST - most homogeneous)

  Interpretation: Very consistent E:I ratios across all parietal subregions.
  Suggests parietal cortex has more uniform E:I balance than other lobes.

KEY FINDING: Occipital and Frontal lobes are most heterogeneous, Parietal
is most homogeneous. This variation occurs WITHIN lobes sharing the same
rCMRGlc value!

================================================================================
PART 2: METABOLIC CLUSTERING ANALYSIS
================================================================================

OBSERVATION: Two distinct metabolic clusters exist

HIGH METABOLISM GROUP (rCMRGlc ~35):
  Lobes: Frontal (35.3), Parietal (35.8), Occipital (35.8)
  Subregions: n = 13
  Mean E:I: 2.58 ± 0.67
  E:I Range: 1.48 - 4.42
  Avg cells/region: 41,120

LOW METABOLISM GROUP (rCMRGlc ~30):
  Lobes: Temporal (30.5), Insular (30.3)
  Subregions: n = 7
  Mean E:I: 2.51 ± 0.45
  E:I Range: 1.73 - 3.13
  Avg cells/region: 45,958

STATISTICAL TEST - Do groups differ in E:I ratio?
  T-test: t = 0.231, p = 0.820
  Mann-Whitney U: p = 0.877

  RESULT: NO significant difference

INTERPRETATION:
  Despite a ~17% difference in metabolism between groups (35 vs 30), there
  is NO difference in E:I ratios. High-metabolism lobes (Frontal, Parietal,
  Occipital) have the same E:I balance as low-metabolism lobes (Temporal,
  Insular).

  This FURTHER SUPPORTS that E:I ratio does NOT correlate with metabolism.

PART 3: ALTERNATIVE ANALYSES
================================================================================

ANALYSIS 1: Excitatory Percentage (instead of E:I ratio)

  Rationale: E:I ratio can be heavily influenced by small changes in
  inhibitory neurons. Excitatory percentage may be more stable.

  Results:
    Correlation with rCMRGlc: r = 0.315, p = 0.176

  CONCLUSION: Still no significant correlation. Excitatory percentage
  explains only 10% of variance (R² = 0.099), same as E:I ratio.

ANALYSIS 2: Within-Lobe E:I Variability

  Rationale: Maybe metabolism relates to E:I *heterogeneity* rather than
  mean E:I?

  Findings:
    Occipital (rCMRGlc=35.8): CV = 25.0% (most variable)
    Frontal (rCMRGlc=35.3):   CV = 20.0%
    Temporal (rCMRGlc=30.5):  CV = 16.2%
    Insular (rCMRGlc=30.3):   CV = 13.5%
    Parietal (rCMRGlc=35.8):  CV = 7.9% (least variable)

  OBSERVATION: No clear pattern. Parietal and Occipital both have
  rCMRGlc=35.8 but vastly different heterogeneity (7.9% vs 25.0%).

ANALYSIS 3: Cell Density

  High metabolism lobes: 41,120 cells/region average
  Low metabolism lobes:  45,958 cells/region average

  FINDING: Low metabolism lobes actually have MORE cells on average!
  This suggests metabolism is not driven by cell density.

ADDITIONAL ANALYSIS SUGGESTIONS
================================================================================

Given the two metabolic clusters (high ~35 vs low ~30), here are further
analyses you could explore:

1. ANATOMICAL/FUNCTIONAL GROUPING
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Instead of E:I, examine what distinguishes high vs low metabolism lobes:

   HYPOTHESIS: Sensory/motor lobes (high) vs associative lobes (low)?

   High metabolism (35):
     - Frontal: Motor (M1C), premotor, prefrontal
     - Parietal: Somatosensory (S1C), posterior parietal
     - Occipital: Visual (V1C, V2)

   Low metabolism (30):
     - Temporal: Auditory (A1C), language, memory
     - Insular: Interoception, emotion

   TEST: Are "primary sensorimotor" regions metabolically higher than
   "associative/limbic" regions?

2. CELL SUBTYPE COMPOSITION
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Rather than just E/I ratio, examine:
   - Parvalbumin+ (fast-spiking) interneurons
   - Somatostatin+ interneurons
   - VIP+ interneurons
   - Excitatory subtypes (IT vs ET vs CT neurons)

   HYPOTHESIS: Metabolic rate may relate to specific interneuron subtypes
   (e.g., PV+ interneurons have high metabolic demand) rather than total
   inhibitory count.

3. LAYER-SPECIFIC E:I RATIOS
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Cortical layers have different E:I balances:
   - Layer 4: High inhibition (granular layer, receives thalamic input)
   - Layers 2/3: High excitatory (cortico-cortical connections)
   - Layers 5/6: Mixed (output layers)

   HYPOTHESIS: High-metabolism regions may have thicker layer 4 or different
   laminar structure affecting E:I.

4. NEURONAL DENSITY vs NEUROPIL
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Metabolism may reflect:
   - Synaptic density (neuropil) rather than cell count
   - Dendritic/axonal arborization
   - Myelination levels

   TEST: Examine genes related to synaptic density, dendritic complexity,
   or myelin in RNA-seq data.

5. METABOLIC GENE EXPRESSION
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Instead of correlating E:I with *measured* metabolism, examine metabolic
   gene expression in the single-cell data:

   Genes to examine:
   - Glycolysis: HK1, PFKM, PKM
   - Oxidative phosphorylation: COX genes, ATP synthase subunits
   - Glucose transporters: SLC2A1 (GLUT1), SLC2A3 (GLUT3)

   HYPOTHESIS: E:I ratio may correlate with cellular expression of metabolic
   genes, even if not with PET-measured regional metabolism.

6. EXCITATORY vs INHIBITORY METABOLIC DEMAND
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Calculate metabolic markers separately for E vs I neurons:
   - Do excitatory neurons in high-metabolism regions express more metabolic
     genes than those in low-metabolism regions?
   - Are inhibitory neurons in high E:I regions more metabolically active?

   TEST: Compare mean expression of metabolic genes in E vs I neurons across
   regions.

7. FIRING RATE PROXIES
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Gene expression proxies for neuronal activity:
   - Immediate early genes: FOS, JUN, ARC, EGR1
   - Activity-regulated genes: NPAS4, NR4A1

   HYPOTHESIS: High E:I regions may have higher baseline activity (more
   transcriptional activity markers), which could explain metabolic demand
   independent of E:I ratio.

8. NETWORK PROPERTIES
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Examine connectivity patterns:
   - Are high-metabolism regions more densely connected?
   - Do they have more long-range vs local connections?
   - Connection to thalamus (high-metabolism) vs limbic (low-metabolism)?

   Data source: Use structural connectivity atlases or tractography

9. VASCULAR/GLIAL FACTORS
   --------------------------------------------------------------------
   Metabolism may be driven by non-neuronal factors:
   - Astrocyte density (support neuronal metabolism)
   - Microglia density (immune/maintenance)
   - Vascular density (blood supply)

   TEST: Examine non-neuronal cell proportions in WHB dataset

10. DEVELOPMENTAL TRAJECTORY
    -------------------------------------------------------------------
    High vs low metabolism lobes may differ in:
    - Developmental timing (early vs late maturing)
    - Neurogenesis patterns
    - Synaptic pruning

    TEST: Examine developmental gene signatures in adult tissue

11. NEUROTRANSMITTER RECEPTOR EXPRESSION
    -------------------------------------------------------------------
    Beyond E/I cell counts, examine receptor expression:
    - AMPA/NMDA receptor subunits (excitatory)
    - GABA-A receptor subunits (inhibitory)
    - Metabotropic receptors

    HYPOTHESIS: Receptor composition (fast vs slow transmission) may
    relate to metabolic demand better than cell counts.

12. CLUSTERING BY E:I RATIO INSTEAD OF METABOLISM
    -------------------------------------------------------------------
    Flip the analysis:
    - Group regions by E:I (high E:I vs low E:I)
    - Compare their metabolism, cell types, gene expression

    QUESTION: What makes high E:I regions different beyond metabolism?

WHY CURRENT ANALYSIS SHOWS NO CORRELATION
================================================================================

The lack of E:I-metabolism correlation likely reflects:

1. MULTI-FACTORIAL METABOLISM:
   Regional metabolism is determined by many factors beyond E/I:
   - Firing rates/activity levels
   - Synaptic density
   - Myelination
   - Non-neuronal cells (glia, vasculature)
   - Metabolic efficiency

2. E/I BALANCE IS TIGHTLY REGULATED:
   All cortical regions maintain similar E:I ratios (2-3:1) for stability.
   Evolution may favor consistent E:I despite varying metabolic demands.

3. METABOLIC CLUSTERING BY FUNCTION:
   The two metabolic groups (35 vs 30) may reflect:
   - Sensorimotor (high) vs associative (low)
   - Externally-driven (high) vs internally-driven (low)
   - Not E/I ratio

4. DATA GRANULARITY MISMATCH:
   We have subregion E:I but only lobe-level metabolism, limiting
   our ability to detect finer relationships.

================================================================================
CONCLUSION
================================================================================

Per-lobe analysis reveals:
  • Substantial E:I variation WITHIN lobes (CV: 7.9% - 25.0%)
  • Occipital and Frontal most heterogeneous, Parietal most homogeneous
  • V1C and M1C are clear outliers within their lobes

Metabolic clustering analysis shows:
  • Two groups: High metabolism (~35) vs Low metabolism (~30)
  • NO difference in E:I between groups (p=0.820)
  • High-metabolism = Frontal/Parietal/Occipital (sensorimotor?)
  • Low-metabolism = Temporal/Insular (associative/limbic?)

Bottom line: E:I ratio does NOT explain metabolic variation, but functional
classification (sensorimotor vs associative) might!
