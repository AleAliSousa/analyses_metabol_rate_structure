INTERPRETATION:
  E:I ratio does NOT reliably predict regional glucose metabolism across

================================================================================
BIOLOGICAL INTERPRETATION
================================================================================

WHY E:I RATIO DOES NOT PREDICT METABOLISM:

1. FUNCTIONAL SPECIALIZATION
   - Regions with similar E:I can have vastly different functions
   - Metabolic demand reflects activity patterns, not just cell counts
   - Example: Pallidum (E:I=0.09) and Basal forebrain (E:I=0.17)
     both inhibitory but different metabolic rates

2. CIRCUIT ARCHITECTURE
   - E/I balance is tightly regulated for network stability
   - Metabolism driven by synaptic activity, not cell type ratios
   - Local circuits can have high activity with balanced E/I

3. REGIONAL SPECIALIZATION
   - Cerebellum: Granule cells create extreme E:I but moderate metabolism
   - Basal ganglia: Inhibitory nuclei with varied metabolic demands
   - Cortex: Relatively uniform E:I but variable metabolism by function

4. GLIAL CONTRIBUTION
   - Glial cells contribute to metabolism but not E:I ratio
   - Astrocyte metabolic support varies by region
   - Oligodendrocytes and myelination affect energy use

3. E/I CLASSIFICATION
   - Based on neurotransmitter markers, not functional activity
   - Some cells may co-express markers
   - Classification rate varied by region

4. METABOLIC DATA
   - rCMRGlc from PET imaging has limited spatial resolution
   - Values represent averages across larger brain regions
   - Some regions lack metabolic measurements

================================================================================
CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

PRIMARY CONCLUSION:
  E:I ratio does NOT significantly correlate with regional glucose
  metabolism across the whole brain (r=0.118, p=0.6107).

SECONDARY FINDINGS:
  - No significant difference in E:I ratios between cortical and subcortical
  - Cortical regions have significantly higher metabolism than subcortical
  - Subcortical structures show much greater E:I variation than cortex
  - Cerebellar structures have extremely high E:I ratios
  - Basal ganglia nuclei are predominantly inhibitory (low E:I)

IMPLICATIONS:
  - Metabolic demand is NOT simply determined by E/I cell type ratios
  - Functional activity patterns and circuit architecture are key
  - Different brain regions use different computational strategies
  - E/I balance is maintained for stability, not metabolic efficiency
